Introduction

Signal integrity (SI) is a critical aspect of PCB design, especially for high-speed digital circuits. It ensures that signals transmitted through PCB traces arrive at their destinations with sufficient quality to be correctly interpreted by receiving components.

Why Signal Integrity Matters
Poor signal integrity can lead to data corruption, timing violations, and system failures. At high frequencies, PCB traces behave like transmission lines, requiring careful design.

Impedance Control

Controlled impedance is essential for high-speed signals. The characteristic impedance of a trace depends on its geometry, dielectric material, and surrounding structures.

Key Factors:

  • Trace width and thickness
  • Dielectric thickness (distance to reference plane)
  • Dielectric constant (εr) of the PCB material
  • Copper thickness

Crosstalk Prevention

Crosstalk occurs when signals on adjacent traces interfere with each other. This can cause signal degradation and errors in digital systems.

Prevention Strategies:

Technique Description Effectiveness 3W Rule Keep traces 3x the trace width apart High Ground Planes Use solid ground planes between signal layers Very High Differential Pairs Route signals as differential pairs Very High Via Stitching Add vias to connect ground planes Medium
Best Practice
For critical signals, use the 3W rule: maintain a spacing of at least 3 times the trace width between adjacent traces to minimize crosstalk.

Signal Routing Strategies

Routing Guidelines:

  • Keep high-speed traces as short as possible
  • Avoid sharp corners (use 45° angles or curves)
  • Maintain consistent trace width
  • Route critical signals on inner layers between ground planes
  • Avoid vias when possible (they introduce impedance discontinuities)
// Example: Trace routing guidelines
- Minimum trace width: 0.1mm (4 mils)
- Minimum spacing: 0.1mm (4 mils)
- Via diameter: 0.2mm (8 mils) minimum
- Via pad: 0.4mm (16 mils) minimum
- Corner radius: 3x trace width
